ghost light /ɡōst līt/ noun

A single bulb left burning center-stage in an empty theatre, so that the space is never truly dark. A safety measure, and a superstition: theatres are said to be haunted, and the light keeps the ghosts at bay.

Intention Wizard
Step 1 of 8 — The foundation

Emotion follows intention.

Human behavior doesn't begin with feeling. It begins somewhere else entirely.

  • Before there is emotion.
  • Before there is action.
  • Before there is performance.

There is imagination. There is intention.

Step 2 of 8 — The same words
"I love you."

Three words. And yet — how many ways can they be performed?

Why are there so many different ways to say the same words?

Step 3 of 8 — The discovery

Because words never determine the performance. The intention behind them does.

You can say "I love you" to:

🫶

Comfort

👁

Manipulate

🙏

Apologize

🔥

Seduce

🛡

Your intention

Step 4 of 8 — The insight

Same words. Different intentions. Different performances.

Great acting doesn't begin with: "How should I feel?"

It begins with understanding why your character is communicating in the first place.

Emotions are a byproduct — the exhaust of genuine intention pursued through imagination. Chase the feeling and you perform a demonstration. Chase the intention and the feeling arrives on its own.

Step 5 of 8 — Finding the intention

There is no single right intention waiting to be discovered. There's the most useful one — the one that makes the scene live.

Start here:

Step 1

Inhabit the given circumstances. Where are you? What just happened? What do you believe is true right now?

Step 2

Ask: what does my character need the other person to do, feel, or believe by the end of this scene?

Step 3

Make it specific. "I need him to admit he was wrong so I can stop feeling like I'm losing my mind" beats "I want him to understand."

Step 4

Test it against the text. A good intention makes the scene harder, not easier — it should create genuine obstacles.

Intentions are transitive — they land on the other person. "I feel guilty" is a state. "I need her to forgive me" is an intention. States are passive. Intentions are active.

Step 6 of 8 — Tactics

Intentions don't stay static. You try something — it doesn't work — you try something else.

These are called tactics: the different ways you pursue the same underlying want.

Say your intention is: "I need her to trust me." You might try:

Charm

Make her want to trust you.

Reason

Give her evidence. Logic.

Plead

Appeal to her sympathy.

Provoke

Shake her into responding.

Confess

Disarm her with honesty.

Withdraw

Make her come to you.

If you play the same thing at the same intensity from top to bottom, you've locked onto a result, not a live intention. Stay responsive. Let your partner's behavior change your tactics — not your want.

Step 7 of 8 — The "as if" door

If the scene's circumstances feel distant or abstract, run them through an as if.

You don't need to have been betrayed by a king to play Hamlet. But you've probably known what it feels like when everyone around you is pretending something false is true. Find the bridge from your life to theirs.

Try it now. Fill in what you know about your scene and let the door open.

Your "as if"

Step 8 of 8 — Your practice

Every time you approach a line, a scene, a role — work through this sequence.

1
What are my given circumstances? What do I know to be true right now?
2
What am I trying to do to the other person?
3
What matters to me — specifically, personally, urgently?
4
When in my life have I needed something like this? (The "as if" door.)
5
What tactics am I using — and what happens when they don't work?

Human behavior is driven by imagination and intention. So is great acting.

A Dossier

GhostLight AI.

The assistant that sits quietly in every Banquo production. It speaks theatre. It keeps the candle burning while you make the art.

i.

Speaks your scenes.

"When does Act II, Scene 3 break?" Ask it like a stage manager would. It answers in the language of the script, not the language of the database.

Built on your Scene Builder. Knows every act, cue, and cast member by name.
ii.

Drafts document templates.

Donor acknowledgments, tax receipts, volunteer thank-yous, rehearsal reports, all in your voice.

Learns your house style. Never sends the same letter twice.
iii.

Watches the budget.

Flags variance before it becomes a problem. Tells you Props is 12% over before the designer does. Gently.

No surprises at the next board meeting.
iv.

Builds your cue-to-cue.

Pulls the lighting, sound, and practical cues your designers already logged into Scene Builder, and lays them out as a structured cue sheet in script order.

It assembles what your team decided. It doesn't decide for them.
v.

Speaks theatre.

Not "user accounts" and "task lists." Companies, casts, rehearsals, strikes, pickups, load-ins. GhostLight understands what you mean when you say "the Tuesday room," "the strike crew," or "the dry tech." Every prompt, every answer, in the language of the art.

vi.

What it won't touch.

It won't design your set, light your show, or tell you how to block a scene. Those calls are yours, always. GhostLight handles the grant letters, donor records, prop tracking, and paperwork, so you have more time for the parts only a person can do.

GhostLight ships when Banquo does. Take a seat and we'll let you know.
